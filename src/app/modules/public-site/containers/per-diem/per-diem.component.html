Foster Care Per Diem

Funding for children in care.

How are foster parents paid?

It’s important to note that foster parents are not employees and caring for a foster child is not a job. This can be very confusing for newcomers to the child welfare system since caring for a child under any other circumstance where money is received would be a job such as babysitting, Personal Support Work, etc. This is important because while foster parents do have rights, they are not covered by any labour laws both provincially and federally. Also important to note, since the money received is not from employment it is not taxable and is not included in your income tax report – see Foster Parents and Social Assistance, H&R Block.

Foster parents receive funding for every day a child is in their care. This funding is called a “per diem” meaning “for each day”. The per diem is not an income. The foster home receives a per diem for each child.

Example 1: Timmy was placed in the home of Jane and John Jackson on the 1st of December. The Jacksons will begin to collect a per diem for Timmy starting on the 1st of December and will continue to receive that per diem until Timmy leaves the home (there is no per diem on the day he leaves). The per diem will be paid by cheque on a monthly basis on the 20th of the following month.

Example 2: Tara needed temporary care and came to the Newman home on the 15th of January and left the home on the 23rd of January. The per diem cheque would be issued and dated for February 20th for 8 days of care (the day she left the home is not paid).

Example 3: Derek and Angela were living at the Klein home for all of March. The Klein home will receive a cheque dated for April 20th for 62 days of care (31 days X 2 children).

Why do foster parents receive a per diem?

Raising children comes with a number of costs. To name a few main expenses:

  • Food
  • Clothing
  • Bedding
  • School Fees
  • Travel
  • Recreational/Artistic Activities
  • March Break and Summer Camps
  • Gifts (birthdays, holidays, etc.)
  • Misc. (phones, haircuts, toothpaste, allowances, etc.)

The per diem is given to foster homes to cover the cost of these items. Here is a breakdown of how much of the per diem should, at minimum, be used to cover these expenses (these are based on the total for an average 30-day month and actual monthly costs will vary:

Item Amount ($)
Food 540
Clothing 120
School Fees 25
Travel 95
Recreational/Artistic Activities 90
March Break and Summer Camps 65
Gifts 45
Misc. 120
TOTAL 1100
*Food is set higher than average allowing for special dietary needs which may come up and for homes who use organic ingredients.

It is important to note that you may spend more in one month on clothes and less in other months. Same goes for all the other categories. Clothing must be purchased new and of the same quality as your own children, if applicable, with the exception of infant clothing which is in good condition with no stains.

How much is given to cover childcare expenses?

The per diem foster parents receive in Ontario averages between $45 - $70 (remember this is for each day). Safe Harbours recognized that when children are engaged in recreational, artistic and educational opportunities they have better outcomes. These opportunities also come with a cost. Therefore, we offer a premium on the provincial per diem average at $75 - $95. Almost all homes will start at $75 unless they have relevant previous foster care experience and/or extensive training in children’s mental health and trauma.

We also recognize that foster parents require additional training and should be following the policies of Safe Harbours. Foster homes who attend training on a regular basis and are compliant with our policies will receive an additional $5 on their per diem for each child. There is additional funding for relief at $10 on the per diem for each child (see Relief section below, basically relief is time off). The total that the home will receive from the per diem is $75 base + $5 training/compliance bonus + $10 relief funding totalling $90 per day for each child. An average 30-day month will add up to $2700.

You may notice that the per diem foster parents receive is higher than the average cost of caring for a child. This allows foster parents to spend more in certain areas if they wish, or if directed by Safe Harbours (i.e. extra activities, specialized camp, etc.). If the foster parents are providing all of the care items and the child has opportunities to do recreational/artistic opportunities without any concerns they can keep whatever is left over to help with other home costs such as mortgage, property tax, insurance, etc. The amount that is kept is not taxable and foster parents must not try to reduce their child’s expenses to increase the amount left over.

Exceptions

While the per diem is paid to cover childcare expenses there are some costs which are reimbursed or covered by a third party. These are:

  • Dental work
  • Prescriptions (over the counter medications excluded unless prescribed by a physician)
  • Travel beyond 200km per month @ $0.38/km
    • First 200km covered by the per diem
    • To/from appointments
    • To/from dropping off child for visits with family (access visit)
    • NOT for family travel such as going to the movies, Niagara Falls, etc.
  • Partial portion of high cost camps
  • Medical devices
  • Clothing for children who have inadequate amounts upon arrival
    • Create inventory of clothing
    • Identify what is needed (taking seasonal needs into account)
    • Create a rough estimate and ask for approval
    • NOT for clothing beyond the initial arrival unless there are extreme circumstances
  • Dietary needs which are prohibitively expensive
    • Food portion of the per diem will be deducted from the reimbursement
    • This has to be discussed and approved

Relief

Everybody needs a break from their kids once in a while and that includes foster parents. The $10 per day for each child for relief allows foster parents to make arrangements for overnight babysitters or, if needed, professional caregivers to stay at the foster home while the parents can relax and get out of the house. When there is another foster home in the area that is capable of caring for the child then arrangements can be made for the child to stay at the other home for the weekend. The foster parents pay the caregiver directly.

Relief caregivers need to have the same screening and paperwork as support staff. Due to this requirement most relief is given by a child's support worker.

Conclusion

Foster parenting is an important safety net for vulnerable children. While it isn't a job it is a highly rewarding way of life with it's hard challenges and tremendous successes. If you have further questions please contact us.
